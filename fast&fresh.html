Weekly uptake: year-over-year comparison

2025 W1-W16 (surcharged, same calendar window) 2026 W1-W16 (no surcharge) Spike weeks (W04, W08, W15)
The baseline is 2025 W1-W16 (the same 16-week calendar window one year prior) to control for seasonality. Post-period average uptake is 3.5% vs baseline 2.0%, which is a +1.6 ppt lift. The spike weeks (W04 at 8.3%, W08 at 6.3%, W15 at 6.6%) are all driven by the same recipe: Sticky Bang Bang Salmon Bowls. This slot 1029 recipe appeared 4 times in the 2025 baseline at normal uptake (2.1% to 3.5%). This confirms people really want this specific recipe, rather than this just being a price-removal effect. If you exclude these three weeks, the true like-for-like post-period average is 2.7% vs 2.09% baseline. That gives us a cleaner +0.6 ppt lift from the surcharge removal alone. Post-removal ratings dropped consistently across all 16 weeks. The most likely reason is audience dilution at scale. Because order volume tripled, a much wider mix of customers is now cooking this recipe. That includes people who might not be huge fans of salmon or this specific flavor profile. This happens a lot when you remove pricing barriers to get more people to try something. A -0.09 gap is small, but it is worth keeping an eye on. If the gap widens, we might have a real satisfaction problem instead of just a broader audience.
05

Observations and data quality flags

Removing the price barrier drives massive trial Confirmed
Getting rid of the surcharge wiped out a major friction point and led to an 84% jump in first-time recipe orders. Customers (especially newer ones) jump at premium options the moment you drop the extra fee.
Spike weeks belong to a single recipe Explained
All three 2026 spike weeks (W04, W08, W15) point to the same recipe: Sticky Bang Bang Salmon Bowls. This dish showed up 4 times in the 2025 baseline at normal uptake levels. That proves it has high organic demand at slot 1029 no matter the price. The spikes are about the recipe, not the missing fee. If you take those weeks out, the like-for-like post-period average is 2.7% vs a 2.09% baseline. That is a cleaner +0.6 ppt lift from dropping the surcharge.
No experimental control group Methodology gap
We rolled this change out to everyone at the same time. Looking at the same calendar window (2025 W1-W16) helps control for seasonality, but we still cannot separate the price drop from year-over-year demand trends, menu updates, or the novelty of the new UI tag. The trialist vs. control split in 2026-W01 also has some self-selection bias. People who picked this recipe might just care more about food in general. Keep in mind that all these findings are directional.
